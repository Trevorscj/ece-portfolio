Strategic Career Trajectory

In the next five years, I'm aiming to secure a technical role in Very Large Scale Integration (VLSI), focusing specifically on digital and analog design or hardware validation. I recently spent time at Amazon Lab126 running full-coverage RTL validation on advanced System-on-Chips (SoCs). My main goal right now is to channel that verification experience directly into hardware architecture design. Knowing exactly how and why structures fail gives me a massive advantage when it comes to designing highly resilient systems.

Looking ten years out, my plan is to leverage everything I've learned about complex tape-outs, failure analysis, and design methodologies to become an engineering lead in the microelectronics sector. I want to be the person teams look to when navigating complex architectural bottlenecks.

Georgia Tech Academic Thread Integration

To get there, I've strategically aligned my coursework at Georgia Tech with the Circuit Technology and Electronic Devices threads. These tracks cover the heavy math behind semiconductor mechanics, high-frequency parasitics, and logical state transitions—concepts you absolutely need to know to tackle deep-submicron design challenges.

I also heavily supplement my classes by getting involved outside the lecture hall:

Silicon Jackets

This student org lets me practice advanced microelectronics design and verify circuits outside of formal academic labs. It's an incredible community and highly regarded by industry recruiters for producing hands-on talent.

NSBE Mentorship

Being part of the National Society of Black Engineers connects me with industry veterans, helps me plan my career moves, and keeps my technical communication skills sharp.

VIP Configurable Computing

Through this Vertically Integrated Project, I get to design modular, high-throughput FPGA and SoC configurations with direct, real-world guidance from our faculty.
