1. Chip Scale Power & Energy Instrumentation (VIP Consortium)

Role: PCB Design Lead | Fall 2022

Sponsored by NASA-JPL, Duracell, and SOCOM, this consortium focuses on creating and characterizing in operando chip-scale electrochemical double layer (ECDL) supercapacitors. The core idea here is to move away from bulky traditional battery packs and instead utilize a functionalized pseudocapacitive structure coupled with a tailored ionic-liquid electrolyte. All of this is embedded directly within a silicon wafer. The ultimate goal? Powering high-altitude telemetry sensors and mini IoT tracking devices for orbital CubeSat platforms where space and weight are absolute premiums.

As the PCB Design Lead during the Fall 2022 phase, my main contribution revolved around building the nervous system for our testing setup. I designed and tracked a custom printed circuit board (PCB) testing framework meant to capture and log the performance curves of these experimental supercapacitors under intense conditions. Orbital space hardware faces extreme ambient testing profiles—wild temperature swings, vacuum conditions, and high-vibration environments. Because of this, my routing demanded meticulous attention to detail. I had to focus on exact pad placement, trace impedance mitigation to prevent signal reflection, and modular environmental isolation so our sensors wouldn't fry.

To achieve the exact trace impedance required for the high-frequency discharge logging, I spent weeks dialing in the dielectric constants and copper weights in my CAD software. We utilized a 4-layer stackup to ensure solid ground planes, which drastically cut down on electromagnetic interference (EMI) between our analog sensor inputs and the digital logic side. Managing the thermal profile was another massive hurdle. Supercapacitors can dump current incredibly fast, generating highly localized heat. I designed custom thermal relief pads and utilized strategic via stitching to pull that heat away from the sensitive data-logging ICs and distribute it evenly across the board's massive ground plane.

The resulting instrumentation board was packed. It housed real-time temperature monitoring, digital barometric altitude logging, and high-frequency capacitor discharge logging arrays. But the piece of this project I’m most proud of is the automated failover relay. When you are testing experimental hardware, failure isn't just an option; it's practically guaranteed. I built an automated hardware-level switch-over relay circuit that acted as a continuous watchdog. It monitored the system's health metrics in real-time. If it detected any voltage drops below our threshold or any current anomalies, it instantly shifted the load demands over to a conventional power backup source. This failover logic ensured that even if the supercapacitor catastrophically failed inside the thermal vacuum chamber, our active testing data was completely safeguarded.

2. Autonomous Mobile Robotics (AMR) Navigation Stack

Focus: Multi-Layer Autonomy, Path-Planning Optimization, & Control Loops | Spring 2026

This development track summarizes the engineering, deployment, and optimization of a complete navigation, localization, and motion control architecture for a physical autonomous vehicle navigating dynamic environments. We essentially built a self-driving stack from the ground up, moving from low-level motor control all the way up to probabilistic path planning.

I. Reactive Autonomy & Collision Abatement

Our initial efforts centered around keeping the vehicle from destroying itself during testing. We utilized a YDLIDAR X4 Pro system for 2D environmental mapping. I contributed to an automatic braking mechanism that dynamically computed a Time-to-Collision (TTC) profile across individual laser tracking channels. The system evaluated the scalar forward component of vehicular velocity projected onto the vector of each distinct sensor scan ray using a custom dynamic formula:

$$TTC = \frac{x}{v \cdot \cos(\theta)}$$

If any single channel tracking metric dropped past our defined safety bounds, the node published immediate deceleration commands to clear risks. But stopping isn't enough; the car needs to navigate. We coupled the braking with a reactive gap-following routine. This algorithm applied disparity field extensions to essentially 'inflate' obstacles in the car's mind, creating safety indexing bubbles. This allowed the vehicle to dynamically negotiate narrow corridors, evaluate the deepest gaps in its field of view, and map local escape routes without scraping against walls.

II. Probabilistic Localization & Motion Stability

Wheel odometry is notoriously unreliable in the real world due to wheel slip and mechanical slop. To overcome this tracking noise, our team set up a structural Monte Carlo Particle Filter (MCPF) localization engine. The node maintained an array of hypothetical poses (particles) across the environment, scoring them in real-time against our pre-mapped occupancy grid using the live LiDAR scans.

Tuning this was an intense exercise in balancing computational load with accuracy. By fine-tuning the sensor weights and probabilistic motion calculations, the car could confidently estimate its position within a 3-4 pixel margin of ground truth. Once we knew exactly where the car was, we tied this position feedback into our control models. I spent days empirically tuning system gains across proportional-derivative (PD), Pure Pursuit, and Stanley control models. Dialing in the derivative gain ($K_d$) was absolutely crucial to resolve transient steering oscillations—often called 'fishtailing'—allowing the vehicle to achieve remarkably smooth and aggressive path tracking.

III. Path Planning Extensions

The top layer of our stack involved calculating trajectories across discrete and continuous spaces. We experimented heavily with $A^*$ search and Rapidly Exploring Random Trees (RRT) algorithms. While our grid-based $A^*$ yielded consistent trajectories, baseline continuous-space RRT suffered from erratic path selection due to its randomized sampling.

To stabilize convergence rates on physical hardware, I built a real-time ray-casting check into the code loop. The algorithm evaluated straight-line visibility options between newly appended branches and the absolute goal position. If the trajectory path was unobstructed by map obstacles, the system bypassed further randomized branching, drawing a line straight to the target and terminating the search. This optimization was a game-changer. It dramatically minimized our computational runtime and completely slashed the erratic, jittery steering behavior that standard RRT usually produces on physical steering servos. The final integrated stack proved highly robust, successfully navigating a maze of dynamic obstacles at speed.
